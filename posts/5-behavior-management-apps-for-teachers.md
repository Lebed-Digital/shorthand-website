If you open the app store and search for classroom tools, you will find hundreds of behavior management apps for teachers. Some promise to gamify your classroom, others promise to automate your parent contacts, and a few claim they will solve all your behavior problems overnight.

As a veteran K-8 teacher and former Registered Behavior Technician, I am naturally skeptical. I have seen apps come and go, and I know that no software can replace good relationship-building and consistent classroom routines.

However, the right tool can make your life a lot easier. It can save you hours of documentation time and keep you from drowning in paper sticky notes, which usually end up stuck to the bottom of my shoe by dismissal.

The trick is knowing what you actually need.

Below is an honest, practical review of five popular behavior management apps. I have categorized them by what they do best, including point systems, parent communication, and individual documentation.

## What to Look For in a Behavior App

Before downloading a new tool, ask yourself what problem you are trying to solve.

Are you looking to motivate primary students with a fun reward system? Do you need a simple messaging app to send announcements? Or are you trying to build a secure paper trail for IEP meetings?

If you are just starting to organize your data collection, it is often best to begin with a paper system first. You can check out our [free behavior log template for teachers](/blog/free-behavior-log-template-for-teachers) to get a feel for what details are worth tracking.

Once you know your goals, you can choose a tool that fits. Here is a quick comparison, followed by a full breakdown of each option.

## 5 Behavior Management Apps for Teachers

### 1. ClassDojo (Best for Elementary Points and Community)

ClassDojo is the giant in the classroom management space. It allows teachers to create cute monster avatars for students and award points for positive behaviors like teamwork or participation. It also has a built-in messaging portal and a "Class Story" feed where you can share photos of class activities.

- **Pros:** Highly engaging for young students. Parents love seeing real-time points and photos of their kids. It is very easy to set up and use.
- **Cons:** The point system can feel juvenile for older elementary and middle schoolers. Additionally, tracking negative points publicly can cause anxiety for some students and lead to defensiveness from parents.

### 2. Remind (Best for Simple Parent Communication)

Remind is not strictly a behavior app, but it is one of the most popular tools for managing student conduct. It is a secure messaging platform that allows teachers to text parents without sharing their personal phone numbers.

- **Pros:** Exceptional delivery rates. Parents are much more likely to read a text message than a long email. You can schedule announcements and send quick check-ins during the day.
- **Cons:** It is purely a communication tool. It does not track behavior data, save incident reports, or help you identify behavioral patterns over time. If you need templates to help structure your messages on these platforms, you can check out our [positive behavior email to parents template](/blog/positive-behavior-email-to-parents-template) collection.

### 3. PBIS Rewards (Best for School-Wide Frameworks)

PBIS Rewards is a digital platform designed for schools implementing Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports. It allows staff members to award points or school-wide currency to students using smartphones or tablets, which can then be redeemed at a school store.

- **Pros:** Great for school-wide consistency. It encourages all staff members, from bus drivers to cafeteria workers, to reinforce positive behaviors.
- **Cons:** It requires school-wide adoption and can be expensive. For an individual teacher looking to manage their own classroom, the setup is far too complex and restrictive.

### 4. LiveSchool (Best for Middle and High School Economies)

LiveSchool is a point-tracking app designed specifically for older students. It allows middle and high schools to run token economies, track behaviors across different classes, and manage school stores.

- **Pros:** The interface is much more mature than ClassDojo, making it acceptable for middle schoolers. It provides excellent data reports for grade-level teams.
- **Cons:** Like PBIS Rewards, it is designed for school-wide or team-wide use. If you are the only teacher using it in your building, the utility is limited.

### 5. ShortHand (Best for Private Behavior Documentation)

ShortHand is a documentation-focused tool designed for individual teachers. Unlike public point systems or general messaging apps, ShortHand is a private assistant that helps you log behavior incidents, track accommodations, and draft polished communication home.

- **Pros:** Built for the individual teacher workflow. It takes seconds to log an entry. It does not require school-wide adoption, and it keeps your records secure and private.
- **Cons:** It does not feature public student avatars or gamified student leaderboards.

## How to Combine Tools Without Burning Out

You do not need to choose just one app, and you do not need to replace the tools your school already mandates. Many teachers use a combination.

For example, you might use ClassDojo to keep your second graders motivated, but use ShortHand privately to log the detailed behavior data you need for IEP goals and to draft professional parent updates.

By using ShortHand alongside your existing tools, you get the best of both worlds. You keep your classroom fun and interactive, while maintaining a secure, searchable paper trail that protects your sanity during report card season.
